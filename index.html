The attractive pressureless Euler-Poisson system describes the evolution of a mass distribution with an initial velocity and gravitational potential.

$$ \begin{align} \begin{cases} \partial _{t}\rho + \partial _{y}(\rho v) =0 \\ \partial _{t}(\rho v)+ \partial _{y}(\rho v^{2}) = -\frac{1}{2}(\text{sgn}* \rho)\rho \end{cases} & & \text{ where } & & \text{sgn}(y)= \begin{cases} -1 & y<0 \\ 0 & y=0 \\ 1 & y>0. \end{cases} \end{align} $$

The study of Sticky Particles solutions (SPS) involves the interaction of point masses undergoing inelastic collisions, hence forming a new particle whose mass equals the sum of all collided particles. The state of the system at time \( t \geq 0 \) is described by \( \psi _{i,t}=(m_{i},y_{i,t},v_{i,t}) \) where \( y_{i,t}=y_{i+1,t} \) corresponds to a pair of already-collided particles.

Adaptive Mesh Refinement Criteria Research August 2024 - May 2025

Developed a Python script to assess the accuracy of an AMR approximation model for the 1D advection equation.

Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR) is a numerical approximation method used in computational simulations. Here, only specific regions of interest use higher resolution, while compensating for accuracy in other regions.

We study AMR solutions to the 1D advection equation described by

$$ \begin{align} \frac{ \partial u }{ \partial t } = -v \frac{ \partial u }{ \partial x } , & & \text{ where } u=u(x,t), x \in [-1,1] \text{ and }t \in [0,1]. \end{align} $$

We approximate \(u\) over a hierarchical grid subdivided into cells \(C_{k_{b}}\) and call that approximation \( u_{\text{apx}}\). Our study consisted of two criteria for refinement,

$$ \begin{align} S_{1}= \max \{ u(x) : x \in C_{k_{b}} \} & & S_{2}= \frac{\max \{ \text{jump_left}, \text{jump_right} \}}{\text{avg_jump} + \varepsilon} \end{align} $$


a threshold value \(c\), and an operating script.

$$ \begin{align} &S_{n}\geq c \implies \text{refine } C_{k_{b}} \\ &S_{n}< c \implies \text{coarsen } C_{k_{b}}\text{ or do nothing.} \end{align} $$

After running \(u_{\text{apx}}\) through \(t \in [0,1]\), we evaluate the accuracy via \(L^{2}=\|u(1)-u_{\text{apx}}(1)\|^{2}\) and taking into account the average total number of cells used in the entire simulation.
